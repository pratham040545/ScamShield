SCAMSHIELD KNOWLEDGE BASE — PHISHING EMAILS
================================================================
This file teaches the AI how phishing emails work.
It covers techniques attackers use, red flags to detect,
and Indian-specific phishing patterns.

=== SECTION 1: SENDER SPOOFING ===

Phishing emails disguise who they're really from using three techniques.

LEVEL 1 — DISPLAY NAME SPOOFING:
The sender's display name shows a trusted brand like "SBI Bank" or
"Income Tax Department" but the actual email address is completely
unrelated — often a free Gmail or Yahoo account.
Example: Display name "HDFC Bank Alert" but actual email is hdfc.alert2024@gmail.com
How to spot: Always click the sender name to reveal the full email address.

LEVEL 2 — LOOKALIKE DOMAIN SPOOFING:
Attackers register domains that look almost identical to real ones.
Techniques used:
- Typosquatting: sbiionline.com (extra i), amazom.com (m instead of n)
- Subdomain tricks: sbi.co.in.secure-login.com (real domain is secure-login.com)
- Hyphenation: sbi-netbanking.com, hdfc-alert.net
- Wrong TLD: incometax.gov.in.com, irctc.co.com
Rule: The real domain is always the part just before the first /
      In "sbi.co.in.secure-login.com/verify" → real domain is "secure-login.com"

LEVEL 3 — EMAIL HEADER SPOOFING:
Advanced attackers forge the actual From: field in email headers.
The email appears to come from support@sbi.co.in exactly.
Only detectable by checking SPF, DKIM, DMARC records in email headers.
Legitimate bank emails always pass SPF and DKIM checks.
In Gmail: click three dots → Show Original → check "PASS" next to SPF and DKIM.

REAL INDIAN EXAMPLES:
- Fake: support@sbi-online-banking.net → Real: support@sbi.co.in
- Fake: noreply@icicibankk.com → Real: noreply@icicibank.com
- Fake: refund@incometax.gov.in.claims.xyz → Real: refund@incometax.gov.in
- Fake: alerts@hdfcbannk.com → Real: alerts@hdfcbank.com

=== SECTION 2: FAKE LINKS AND URL TRICKS ===

Phishing emails always contain a malicious link disguised to look legitimate.
Even technically aware users get fooled because the tricks happen at multiple levels.

TRICK 1 — ANCHOR TEXT MISMATCH:
The visible text of a link says one thing but the actual URL is different.
Example: Email shows "Login to your SBI account → www.sbi.co.in"
         But hovering reveals the real URL: www.sbi-secure-verify.net/login
How to spot: Always hover over any link before clicking.
             The real URL appears in the bottom left corner of your browser.
             On mobile: long press the link to preview the actual URL.

TRICK 2 — SUBDOMAIN CONFUSION:
Attackers put the real brand name as a subdomain prefix to fool victims.
Example: sbi.co.in.account-verify.com
         Victims read "sbi.co.in" and think it's safe.
         But the actual domain is "account-verify.com" — owned by the attacker.
Rule: Read URLs right to left from the first single slash (/).
      Everything before the slash is the domain.
      The real owner is the last two parts before the slash.
      "sbi.co.in.account-verify.com/login" → owner is "account-verify.com"

TRICK 3 — URL SHORTENERS IN OFFICIAL COMMUNICATIONS:
bit.ly, tinyurl.com, t.co, rb.gy used in emails claiming to be from banks or government.
Real banks, UIDAI, Income Tax Department NEVER use URL shorteners in official emails.
Any shortened URL in a financial or government email is a red flag.
How to expand: paste the short URL into checkshorturl.com before clicking.

TRICK 4 — HOMOGRAPH / UNICODE ATTACKS:
Some characters in other languages look visually identical to English letters.
Cyrillic 'а' looks exactly like Latin 'a' but is a completely different character.
Examples:
- pаypal.com (Cyrillic а) vs paypal.com (Latin a)
- аmazon.com (Cyrillic а) vs amazon.com (Latin a)
How to spot: Copy the URL and paste into Notepad — sometimes rendering differs.
             Use VirusTotal.com to scan any suspicious URL.

TRICK 5 — HTTP INSTEAD OF HTTPS:
Any page asking for login, OTP, card details, or Aadhaar over HTTP is dangerous.
Real bank and government pages always use HTTPS (padlock icon in browser).
Important: HTTPS alone does not mean the site is safe — phishing sites also
           get SSL certificates. Check BOTH the padlock AND the domain name.

REAL INDIAN PHISHING LINK EXAMPLES:
- http://sbi-online-verify.com/login (HTTP + fake domain)
- https://sbi.co.in.update-kyc.net/account (subdomain trick)
- bit.ly/hdfc-kyc-urgent (URL shortener in "bank" email)
- https://incometax-refund.xyz/claim (wrong TLD, not .gov.in)
- https://аmazon.in/offers (homograph attack — Cyrillic а)

=== SECTION 3: URGENCY AND FEAR TACTICS ===

Phishing emails work by bypassing logical thinking through psychological manipulation.
Attackers create panic so victims act before they think.
Understanding these triggers helps identify phishing even when technical signs are subtle.

TRIGGER 1 — FEAR OF LOSS:
Phishing emails threaten to take away something the victim values.
Common fear-of-loss phrases:
- "Your SBI account has been temporarily suspended"
- "Your Aadhaar card will be permanently deactivated within 48 hours"
- "Failure to update KYC will result in account closure"
- "Your PAN card has been blocked due to suspicious activity"
- "Your EPF account has been frozen"
Why it works: Fear of losing access to money or identity documents triggers
              immediate panic, overriding rational thinking.
Reality check: Banks and government agencies send official letters for serious
               account actions. They do not threaten via email with 24-hour deadlines.

TRIGGER 2 — ARTIFICIAL DEADLINES:
Phishing emails always include a tight deadline to prevent victims from verifying.
Common deadline phrases:
- "Act within 24 hours or your account will be closed permanently"
- "This link expires in 2 hours"
- "You must respond before midnight tonight"
- "Final warning — last chance to update your details"
Why it works: Deadlines create time pressure that stops victims from
              calling the bank, checking the official website, or asking someone.
Reality check: No legitimate financial institution closes accounts
               within 24 hours via email without prior written notice.

TRIGGER 3 — AUTHORITY IMPERSONATION:
Phishing emails impersonate high-authority institutions to make victims comply without questioning.
Commonly impersonated Indian authorities:
- Reserve Bank of India (RBI)
- Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI)
- Income Tax Department
- UIDAI / Aadhaar Authority
- Enforcement Directorate (ED)
- Supreme Court of India
- TRAI (Telecom Regulatory Authority)
Common authority phrases:
- "This is an official communication from RBI"
- "CBI has registered a case against your PAN number"
- "Income Tax Department has detected discrepancies in your returns"
- "TRAI will disconnect all your mobile numbers within 2 hours"
Why it works: People are conditioned to obey authority figures immediately,
              especially when legal consequences are threatened.
Reality check: RBI never emails individuals directly about account issues.
               CBI/police never warn suspects via email before arrest.
               Government agencies communicate via official letters, not Gmail.

TRIGGER 4 — PRIZE AND REWARD BAIT:
Opposite of fear — excitement about unexpected gain lowers critical thinking.
Common prize phrases:
- "Congratulations! Your email was selected in KBC Lucky Draw"
- "You have won Rs 25 lakh in Jio Anniversary Offer"
- "Amazon India has selected you for a free iPhone 15"
- "Your SBI account has an unclaimed reward of Rs 8,500"
Why it works: Excitement and greed bypass skepticism just like fear does.
Reality check: You cannot win a lottery or prize you never entered.
               All prize claims requiring upfront fees are 100% scams.

COMBINED ATTACK — FEAR + AUTHORITY + DEADLINE:
Most sophisticated phishing emails combine all three triggers together.
Example: "URGENT: RBI has detected suspicious transactions on your account.
          Your account will be permanently blocked within 24 hours.
          Click here immediately to verify your identity and avoid legal action."
This single message uses authority (RBI), fear (account blocked, legal action),
and deadline (24 hours) simultaneously — making it extremely effective.

HOW TO BREAK THE PANIC:
- Stop. Take 3 deep breaths before clicking anything.
- Ask: Did I initiate this interaction? If no → suspicious.
- Verify directly: Open a new browser tab, type the official website manually.
- Call the official customer care number from the back of your card or official website.
- Remember: Legitimate organizations give you days or weeks, not hours.

=== SECTION 4: CREDENTIAL HARVESTING ===

Credential harvesting is the primary goal of most phishing attacks.
After tricking the victim into clicking a malicious link, attackers
capture login credentials, OTPs, card details, or identity information
through fake websites that look identical to legitimate ones.

HOW FAKE LOGIN PAGES WORK:
Attackers create pixel-perfect copies of real bank or government websites.
The fake page looks identical — same logo, colors, fonts, layout.
The only difference is the URL and the fact that everything you type
is sent directly to the attacker's database.
After capturing credentials, the fake page either:
- Shows "Incorrect password, please try again" (to capture typing variations)
- Shows "Session expired" and redirects to the real website
- Pretends to process and then shows a fake success message
The victim assumes nothing suspicious happened.

WHAT ATTACKERS STEAL:
Tier 1 — Account credentials:
- Net banking username and password
- Email account login
- Social media account login

Tier 2 — Financial details:
- Debit/credit card number (16 digits)
- Card expiry date
- CVV number (3 digits on back)
- ATM PIN

Tier 3 — Identity documents:
- Aadhaar number + date of birth
- PAN card number
- Passport number
- Mother's maiden name (security question answer)

Tier 4 — One Time Passwords:
- Net banking OTP
- UPI transaction OTP
- Email/phone verification OTP
CRITICAL: Entering OTP on a fake page gives attacker 30-60 seconds
          to use it on the real website before it expires.

ADVERSARY IN THE MIDDLE (AiTM) ATTACK:
Advanced phishing that defeats even two-factor authentication.
How it works:
- Victim visits fake site that acts as a proxy for the real bank
- Every input (username, password, OTP) is forwarded to real bank
- Real bank's response is forwarded back to victim
- Victim sees a completely normal login experience
- Attacker captures session cookies — bypassing OTP entirely
- With session cookies, attacker stays logged in even after OTP expires
Signs: Slight loading delay, URL is wrong, sometimes minor visual glitches.

FAKE KYC UPDATE PAGES:
Very common in India targeting Paytm, PhonePe, UIDAI, and bank customers.
Fake page asks to "re-verify" identity by entering:
- Aadhaar number + OTP sent to registered mobile
- PAN number + date of birth
- Bank account number + IFSC code
Once submitted, attacker has everything needed for identity fraud,
opening fake bank accounts, or taking loans in victim's name.

RECOGNIZING FAKE PAGES BEFORE ENTERING DETAILS:
1. Check the URL FIRST — before looking at anything else on the page
2. Look for HTTPS padlock — necessary but not sufficient
3. Check the exact domain — not just that it contains the bank name
4. Look for poor quality logos, slightly wrong fonts, broken images
5. Never trust a page reached by clicking an email or SMS link
6. When in doubt: close the tab, open a new one, type URL manually

WHAT TO DO IF YOU ALREADY ENTERED CREDENTIALS:
1. Immediately call your bank's official helpline (number on card back)
2. Change your net banking password from a different device
3. Block your debit/credit card via official app or helpline
4. Check last 10 transactions for unauthorized activity
5. File complaint on cybercrime.gov.in or call 1930 (cyber crime helpline)
6. If Aadhaar was compromised: lock Aadhaar biometrics at myaadhaar.uidai.gov.in

=== SECTION 5: INDIAN CONTEXT PHISHING ===

Indian phishing attacks exploit institutions, fears, and cultural contexts
specific to India. These attacks are highly effective because they target
trust in well-known Indian government schemes and financial institutions.

GOVERNMENT SCHEME PHISHING:
Attackers impersonate popular Indian government schemes to steal bank details.
Common schemes exploited:
- PM Kisan Samman Nidhi: "Your Rs 2,000 installment is pending. Verify bank account."
- Ayushman Bharat: "Your free health insurance card is ready. Click to activate."
- PM Awas Yojana: "Your housing loan subsidy of Rs 2.67 lakh is approved. Claim now."
- PM Ujjwala Yojana: "Free LPG connection approved. Submit Aadhaar to confirm."
Why effective: Genuine beneficiaries of these schemes exist in large numbers.
               Victims believe it's real because they may actually be eligible.
Red flag: Real scheme benefits go directly to Aadhaar-linked bank accounts.
          No scheme requires you to click a link to "claim" money.

AADHAAR AND UIDAI PHISHING:
Aadhaar is linked to everything in India — making Aadhaar threats extremely scary.
Common Aadhaar phishing messages:
- "Your Aadhaar card will be permanently deactivated within 24 hours.
   Update your mobile number immediately: [fake link]"
- "UIDAI has detected your Aadhaar being used in 3 states simultaneously.
   Verify your identity to prevent misuse."
- "Your Aadhaar KYC is expired. Re-verify to continue using government services."
Reality: UIDAI never sends emails asking to click links for Aadhaar verification.
         Aadhaar updates happen only at official Aadhaar centres or myaadhaar.uidai.gov.in
         Aadhaar is never "deactivated" via email notice.

INCOME TAX REFUND PHISHING:
One of the most common and effective Indian phishing attacks.
Sample fake email:
"Dear Taxpayer, Income Tax Department has processed your refund of Rs 18,540.
 To receive the refund in your bank account, please verify your account details
 by clicking the link below within 7 working days.
 Link: http://incometax-refund-claim.xyz/verify"
Why effective: Many Indians file ITR and genuinely expect refunds.
Reality: Income Tax refunds go automatically to the bank account linked with PAN.
         The IT department never asks you to "verify" or "claim" via email link.
         Official IT dept domain: incometax.gov.in — anything else is fake.

IRCTC AND RAILWAY PHISHING:
India has 23 million daily railway passengers — huge target base.
Common IRCTC phishing:
- "Your IRCTC ticket booking has failed. Refund of Rs 1,240 pending.
   Click to claim: http://irctc-refund.net/claim"
- "IRCTC is offering 50% discount on Tatkal bookings for Diwali.
   Book now: http://irctc-diwali-offer.com"
Reality: IRCTC refunds are automatic to original payment source.
         Official domain only: irctc.co.in — no other domain is official.

EPF AND PENSION PHISHING:
Targets salaried employees and retirees.
Common EPF phishing:
- "Your EPF withdrawal request of Rs 85,000 is stuck due to KYC mismatch.
   Update your Aadhaar-UAN link: [fake link]"
- "EPFO: Your pension amount has been revised. Verify bank details to receive."
Reality: EPF updates happen only on epfindia.gov.in or official EPFO app.
         UAN portal login: unifiedportal-mem.epfindia.gov.in

DIGITAL ARREST SCAM EMAILS:
Sophisticated scam combining phishing with impersonation of law enforcement.
Sample email:
"This is an official notice from Cyber Crime Division, CBI.
 Your email address has been found linked to an international money laundering case.
 You are required to appear for digital verification within 24 hours.
 Failure to respond will result in immediate arrest warrant.
 Contact investigating officer: [fake number or link]"
Reality: CBI, ED, and police NEVER send arrest warnings via email.
         There is no such thing as "digital arrest" — it is entirely fabricated.
         Real legal notices are served physically with court stamps and signatures.

FESTIVE SEASON PHISHING (HIGH RISK PERIODS):
Phishing attacks surge during Indian festivals and events.
Diwali: Fake shopping offers, fake cashback, fake gift card generators
IPL Season: Fake match ticket offers, fake fantasy cricket winning notifications
Budget Season: Fake tax saving scheme emails, fake insurance offers
COVID/Health Crisis: Fake vaccination slots, fake health insurance offers
Election Season: Fake voter ID update requests, fake ECI communications

HINGLISH PHISHING (HINDI + ENGLISH MIX):
Some phishing messages use Hinglish to appear more local and trustworthy.
Examples:
- "Aapka SBI account 24 ghante mein band ho jayega. Abhi verify karein."
- "Congratulations! Aapne Rs 50,000 jeeta hai. Claim karne ke liye click karein."
- "Aapka Aadhaar card suspend ho gaya hai. Turant update karein."
Red flag: Legitimate bank communications in India use formal language.
          Mixing casual Hindi with urgent financial threats is a scam pattern.

=== SECTION 6: WHAT LEGITIMATE EMAILS LOOK LIKE ===

Understanding what genuine emails look like is as important as
knowing phishing patterns. This prevents false positives — flagging
real bank or government emails as suspicious.

LEGITIMATE BANK EMAIL CHARACTERISTICS:

Sender domain:
- SBI: alerts@sbi.co.in, noreply@sbi.co.in
- HDFC: alerts@hdfcbank.com, noreply@hdfcbank.com
- ICICI: alerts@icicibank.com
- Axis Bank: alerts@axisbank.com
- Kotak: noreply@kotak.com
Rule: Official bank emails ALWAYS come from the bank's own domain.
      Never from Gmail, Yahoo, Hotmail, or lookalike domains.

Language and tone:
- Addresses you by your FULL NAME as registered with the bank
- Formal, grammatically correct English
- No spelling mistakes or odd capitalization
- Does not use casual language or Hinglish

What legitimate bank emails DO:
- Inform you of transactions already completed
- Send OTP for transactions YOU initiated
- Notify about statement availability
- Confirm changes YOU made to your account
- Send scheduled promotional offers with unsubscribe option

What legitimate bank emails NEVER do:
- Ask for your password, PIN, or OTP
- Ask you to click a link to "verify" or "update" your account
- Threaten account closure within 24-48 hours
- Ask for card number, CVV, or expiry date
- Request Aadhaar or PAN via email
- Use URL shorteners (bit.ly, tinyurl)
- Send attachments asking you to "enable macros"

LEGITIMATE GOVERNMENT EMAIL CHARACTERISTICS:

Official Indian government email domains:
- Income Tax: donotreply@incometax.gov.in
- UIDAI: help@uidai.gov.in
- EPFO: epfindia@epfindia.gov.in
- RBI: only communicates via official notifications on rbi.org.in
- IRCTC: care@irctc.co.in

What legitimate government emails DO:
- Confirm transactions or applications you submitted
- Send informational newsletters you subscribed to
- Notify about policy changes publicly
- Direct you to official .gov.in domains only

What legitimate government emails NEVER do:
- Ask you to pay fees via UPI or link
- Threaten arrest or legal action via email
- Ask you to call a personal mobile number
- Request Aadhaar OTP via email
- Use free email domains like Gmail

TECHNICAL MARKERS OF LEGITIMATE EMAILS:
SPF Check: PASS (verifies email came from authorized server)
DKIM Check: PASS (verifies email wasn't tampered in transit)
DMARC: PASS (domain owner authorized this email)
How to check in Gmail:
1. Open email → click three dots (top right) → Show Original
2. Look for SPF: PASS, DKIM: PASS, DMARC: PASS at the top
3. Any FAIL here = treat email as suspicious regardless of appearance

SAFE EMAIL EXAMPLE — SBI TRANSACTION ALERT:
From: alerts@sbi.co.in
Subject: Transaction Alert - Your SBI Account
Body: "Dear [Your Full Name],
      A debit of Rs 2,500.00 has been made from your account
      ending XXXX on [date] at [merchant].
      Available balance: Rs XX,XXX.XX
      If you did not authorize this transaction, call 1800-11-2211"
Why this is safe:
- Comes from official sbi.co.in domain
- Addresses you by full name
- Shows only last 4 digits of account (not full number)
- Contains no links asking for credentials
- Provides official phone number for disputes

WHEN YOU ARE UNSURE — SAFE VERIFICATION STEPS:
1. Do NOT click any link in the email
2. Open a new browser tab
3. Type the official website URL manually
4. Log in and check if the alert/offer/notification exists there
5. If it doesn't exist on the real site → the email is phishing
6. Call official customer care (number from official website or card back)
   Never call numbers mentioned in the suspicious email itself
